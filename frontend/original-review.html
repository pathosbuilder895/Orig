What do these signals mean? — tap to expand explanations for flagged features
Each feature below measures a distinct dimension of writing style. A score far from baseline means this submission's patterns differ notably from how this student normally writes — not that the student did not write it.
Type-Token Ratio (TTR) −43%
TTR measures vocabulary diversity — the proportion of unique words relative to total words. A lower TTR means the text repeats words more often. This student normally uses rich, varied vocabulary (TTR 0.72). This submission is more repetitive (0.41), consistent with either a more formulaic writing style, a topic requiring technical repetition, or text generated by someone else with different vocabulary habits.
Hapax Legomena Rate −43%
Hapax legomena are words that appear exactly once in a text — a strong signal of breadth of expression. This student typically uses many such words (0.49). This submission uses far fewer (0.28), suggesting a narrower range of vocabulary choices than is characteristic of this writer.
Mean Sentence Length +71%
Sentence length is a highly consistent stylometric marker. This student typically writes in sentences averaging 18.3 words. This submission averages 31.2 words — significantly longer, and notably more formal in construction. Possible explanations: the student was writing in an unfamiliar register (academic theology), was emulating a source text, or the text was produced by a writer who favors longer sentences.
Function Word Ratio −34%
Function words (the, of, in, a, with…) are used largely unconsciously and are among the most stable stylometric signals. A reduced function word ratio often indicates a shift in syntactic construction style. This is difficult to consciously reproduce and is one of the stronger signals in this report.
Passive Voice Ratio +158%
This student rarely uses passive voice (baseline 0.12). This submission uses it at 2.6× the typical rate (0.31). Academic theology writing often favors passive constructions ("it is argued that…", "is to be understood as…") — which may partly explain the difference. However, the magnitude of the shift warrants attention.

The doctrine of Christology occupies a position of unparalleled centrality within the systematic theological enterprise, insofar as the hypostatic union — that metaphysical conjunction of the divine and human natures within the singular person of Jesus Christ — constitutes the very foundation upon which soteriological consideration necessarily depends. It is argued within this essay that a robust Chalcedonian framework provides the most adequate account of the biblical witness concerning the person of Christ.

The Nicene formulation, consolidated at the Council of Constantinople in 381 AD, established with precision the doctrinal parameters within which orthodox Christological reflection has since proceeded. The homoousios — of one substance with the Father — is to be understood not merely as a conciliar political compromise but as a genuine metaphysical claim regarding the ontological identity of the Second Person of the Trinity with the divine essence.

Subsequent reflection within the Chalcedonian tradition has sought to articulate the manner in which the two natures are united without confusion, change, division, or separation — the four negative adverbs of the Definition operating as a kind of apophatic boundary marking what orthodox confession excludes rather than positively specifying the mode of union.
