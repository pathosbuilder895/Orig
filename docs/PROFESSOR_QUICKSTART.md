# Original + Bluebook — Professor Quickstart

*Three pages. Everything you need for the semester.*

## What this is — and what it is not

Original learns each student's **writing voice** — 103 measurable habits of
vocabulary, rhythm, punctuation, argument structure, and (in proctored
examinations) typing dynamics — from work you trust, and then tells you how
closely later submissions match that voice.

**What it is not:** a verdict machine. From the model card, verbatim:
*"A recommendation is never equivalent to a finding of misconduct."* The
system's calibration is honest about its limits — a flag means *look closer
and talk to the student*, never *punish*. No score in this system should ever
be the sole evidence in an integrity determination, and nothing is ever
auto-penalized.

## Week 1 — collect the baseline (the most important week)

A profile needs **at least 3 trusted writing samples** per student (5+ is
better; escalation recommendations are suppressed below 5).

The strongest baseline is a **proctored Bluebook sitting**:

1. Open **Bluebook → Examinations → + New Examination**. Give it a title
   ("Week 1 Writing Sample"), 20–30 minutes, a low minimum word count
   (150–250), and a reflective prompt students can answer cold.
2. Leave the **Secure Lockdown** toggles ON (the defaults).
3. Students enter through the Canvas link (no separate login). They write
   full-screen; copy/paste is disabled; the session captures typing rhythm —
   all of which is disclosed to them on the briefing screen first.
4. When they **Seal & Surrender**, the sample lands on their profile
   automatically as highest-trust baseline material.

Top up with prior essays you trust via **Import Papers** on the professor
dashboard. **Tell students in advance** — the syllabus disclosure text your
department received covers what's collected and why.

## Week 2 onward — scoring take-home work

Paste (or import) a submission on the professor dashboard → **Analyze
Writing**. You get a deviation score, a plain-English explanation of *which*
habits moved, and one of four recommendations:

| Recommendation | What it means | What you do |
|---|---|---|
| **No action** | matches their voice | nothing |
| **Monitor** | mild drift | note it; drift often = growth |
| **Schedule conversation** | meaningful divergence | a pastoral chat: "walk me through how you wrote this" |
| **Escalate** | strong divergence on a well-established profile | follow your institution's integrity process — *with* other evidence |

In **Bluebook → Results** each sat examination shows two numbers:
- **Stylometric** — how well the typing/style of *this sitting* matches their profile.
- **AI Score** — authorship probability of the text against their baseline (higher = more authentically theirs). Shows "—" until a baseline exists.

## Reading flags wisely

- **New students drift.** First-year voices change fast; that's formation, not fraud.
- **Genre matters.** A poetry exercise scored against sermon baselines will wobble; the context system compensates, but read the explanation panel.
- **Non-native English speakers** can show different stability patterns — the system is monitored for this, and it's one more reason a flag is a conversation, not a conclusion.
- **Lockdown is deterrence, not a guarantee.** A determined student has other devices. The keystroke record and the conversation are your real instruments.

## When something looks wrong

1. Open the submission's explanation panel — which tiers moved, and which direction.
2. Compare against their baseline samples (History → Baselines).
3. Talk with the student. Most conversations end well, and the system is built on that assumption.
4. If it proceeds further, export the audit trail through your admin contact — never screenshot a score as "proof."
